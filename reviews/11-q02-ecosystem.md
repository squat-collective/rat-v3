# Q02 external review (SIMULATED dry-run) — ecosystem / plugin-author
Reviewer persona: a DevRel/integrations lead who has grown a provider ecosystem (Terraform-provider / dbt-adapter / Singer-tap / Backstage-plugin shaped scars) · Lens: ecosystem · Date: 2026-06-02 · Provenance: SIMULATED (AI persona)

## Bottom line

**Would I build a plugin for this? Yes — eventually, and more willingly than for almost any data platform I've seen *on contract design alone*.** The contract triple is the most author-respectful surface in the space: capabilities map 1:1 to RPC methods (`contracts/proto/rat/format/v1/CONTRACT.md:23-35`), per-kind schemas mandate only a minimal core and negotiate the rest (`contracts/schema/kinds/format.v1.json:20-36`), the per-axis `CONTRACT.md` author guides are genuinely good, and "couple by capability, never by peer name" (`plugin.v1.json:104-109`) is cleaner than VSCode's named `extensionDependencies` or dbt's warehouse-bound adapters. reviews/02 feared the contracts wouldn't exist; they do, they're frozen, and they're well-documented. That fear is retired.

**Would I bet a tool's distribution on it *today*? No.** Three reasons, in order. (1) **The last mile an author walks does not exist (F1):** there is no path from `git clone` to a registered, running third-party plugin — the ~32 reference plugins ship *no manifest*, the two detached `plugin.yaml` files are named for plugins that aren't in `examples/`, the manifest can't even express *how to launch the plugin*, and scaffold/validate/dev-loop are backlog. The maintainer **conceded all of this** — the examples are "conformance fixtures, not starter templates," and the missing `examples/README.md` is a real doc-drift regression against ADR-006 D2. (2) **The capability-negotiation moat is coarser than advertised (F3):** the frozen `capabilityRef` is a bare URI with no attributes/selector/version-range — it cannot express "I scan Parquet but not Delta," and the spike *refuses* to choose between two providers of the same capability, punting selection to an operator plane-binding layer that isn't even a frozen contract. (3) **The trust chain that makes a third-party plugin installable is not closed end-to-end (F4):** the core-load attestation check is real and built (credit where due — `core/registry/verified.go`), but *nothing in production issues* the attestation it consumes, and the marketplace install-time trust check is paper. The architecture earned the right to an ecosystem; it has not yet built the on-ramp, the negotiation granularity, or the trust pipeline that turn a great contract into a living market. **None of this reopens the frozen wire** — it's tooling, additive contract fields, and GTM. But "it's all Phase 2/4" is itself the risk: F1, F3, and F4 are cold-start-critical and can't all wait for the architecture-complete milestone.

## Findings

### F1 — The `git clone` → working third-party plugin path is not walkable
- **Severity:** Critical (for cold-start adoption)
- **Area:** ecosystem
- **Finding:** An author who does the obvious thing — copy a reference plugin — gets an incomplete plugin. The ~32 plugins under `examples/**` ship **no `plugin.yaml`** (verified: `examples/format/delta-py/` contains `main.py`/`server.py`/`store.py`/`streams.py`/`harness_test.py`/`requirements.txt` and no manifest). The *only* manifests in the repo are two detached files in `contracts/examples/` (`rat-format-deltalake.plugin.yaml`, `rat-strategy-scd2.plugin.yaml`) — and they are named for plugins that **don't exist as runnable code** (the runnable Delta plugin is `examples/format/delta-py`, the runnable SCD plugins are `scd2-py`/`fullrefresh-py`). There is **no `examples/README.md`** despite ADR-006 D2 explicitly promising one ("`examples/README.md` — how to build/run a reference plugin"; `docs/architecture/adrs/006-...md:91`) — the maintainer confirms this is a genuine **doc-drift regression**, not merely a Phase-4 deferral. No `rat plugin new/validate/test` exists (no `cmd/` at all; backlog ADR-017, `roadmap/backlog.md:108,161-162`).
- **Why it matters:** Friction-to-first-plugin is the single best predictor of whether an ecosystem reaches critical mass — I have watched this kill more platforms than any architectural flaw. The author's day-one model is "copy the closest example, change the logic, ship." Here that yields a gRPC server that passes conformance but **is not a plugin**: no manifest, no declared capabilities, nothing the registry can index, no way to tell RAT how to start it. The author must reverse-engineer the manifest from a *different*, non-co-located, differently-named file, and hand-assemble packaging — a day of undocumented yak-shaving before writing a line of their own logic. Most evaluators bounce inside that day. Cargo/VSCode won precisely because that day is ten minutes.
- **Freeze-reopen?** no (tooling + docs + co-location; the wire is untouched)
- **Cross-lens:** **maintainer CONCEDED in full** — confirmed zero co-located manifests, the 2 detached/misnamed manifests, the missing `examples/README.md` vs ADR-006 D2 (flagged as a discrete fixable drift), and "examples are conformance fixtures, not starter templates; copy-a-reference is incomplete-by-design" (scaffolding is Phase 2).
- **Suggested direction:** Dual-purpose the references as templates *now*: co-locate a real `plugin.yaml` in every `examples/<axis>/<impl>/` dir, ship the promised `examples/README.md` with literal copy→edit→run→conform steps, and pull a thin `rat plugin validate <dir>` (the JSON Schema already exists — a wrapper) forward from ADR-017. Full scaffolding can stay Phase 2; *a complete copyable example cannot*.

### F2 — No documented inner dev loop, and the frozen manifest can't say how to launch the plugin
- **Severity:** High
- **Area:** ecosystem|contracts
- **Finding:** Two coupled gaps. (a) **Dev loop:** `examples/format/delta-py/main.py:16-24` starts a gRPC server on an OS-assigned port and prints it, registering with nothing. The maintainer confirms there is **no documented localhost-attach workflow** and no `rat dev --plugin` (Phase 2). Precise nuance (maintainer-supplied): the spike core's *actual* provisioning (ADR-014 §3) is "dial a pre-running gRPC server," so the mechanism an author wants exists — but only as **test-harness wiring, not an exposed `rat dev` override**; and the ADR-016 `local-process` runtime **execs a binary, not a container**, so a Go plugin doesn't strictly need an OCI image per cycle — but a **Python plugin has no documented attach-or-exec dev path** at all. (b) **Launch metadata is absent from the contract the author writes:** the frozen `plugin.v1.json` has `additionalProperties:false` and **no `image`/`command`/`entrypoint` field** (verified: required set is `api_version,kind,metadata,provides,resources`; no launch field anywhere in `contracts/schema/`), yet ADR-016 §1 says the core "builds a `LaunchSpec` from the manifest (`image`/command…)". The manifest cannot express that today, so packaging/launch lives in an undocumented out-of-band layer.
- **Why it matters:** If iterating means build-image-and-reinstall, iteration is minutes-per-cycle and only paid-to-do-it authors persist (the Eclipse-plugin-churn failure). The mitigating fact (local-process execs binaries) only helps Go authors; the Python references — i.e. most of `examples/` — have no blessed loop. And an author who writes the manifest per the schema, writes the server, then asks "what tells RAT how to *start* my server?" finds no answer in the artifact they were told is the contract.
- **Freeze-reopen?** no — but note: if launch metadata belongs in the manifest, an `image`/`spec` block is an *additive* change within `rat/1` (allowed). Decide before authors hard-code the out-of-band convention.
- **Cross-lens:** **maintainer CONCEDED** ("no ergonomic, documented inner loop today; the spike has dial-pre-running but it's unexposed/test-only"). Corrected my earlier overstatement that every cycle needs an image — true only for container runtimes, not `local-process`.
- **Suggested direction:** Expose the spike's dial-pre-running as a documented `rat dev` localhost override; ship a Python attach/exec dev path; and state in `plugin.v1.json`'s description + a CONTRACT.md whether launch metadata is a (additive) manifest field or an operator-config layer.

### F3 — Capability negotiation is URI-only and attribute-less; multi-provider selection is unspecified
- **Severity:** High
- **Area:** contracts|premise|ecosystem
- **Finding:** (Raised jointly with the architect.) The frozen `capabilityRef` is a bare `{capability: "rat://axis/v1/x"}` with `additionalProperties:false` — **no version range, no per-capability schema, no attributes or discriminator** (`plugin.v1.json:179-185`). The `overview.md` manifest example that shows `provides` with a name+schema and `requires` with `version: ">=v1"` is **fictional** relative to the frozen schema. Real plugins differ on exactly the attributes the URI cannot carry: a `format` that provides `rat://format/v1/scan` *for Parquet only* is indistinguishable from one that scans Delta or Iceberg; an `engine` advertising `query` says nothing about its SQL-dialect subset. Worse, when two plugins provide the same capability, the spike **refuses to choose** (architect-confirmed) and selection falls back to operator **plane-bindings — which are not even a frozen contract artifact.**
- **Why it matters:** The capability-negotiation model is sold as *the* moat — "couple by capability, the deployment auto-answers does-this-work." But attribute-less URIs make the negotiation **over-promise**: `requires: rat://format/v1/scan` will "match" a format that physically can't read the author's tables, and the failure surfaces at runtime inside the *consuming* plugin's name (the strategy author gets blamed for the format author's incapacity — reviews/02 Stage-9 attribution problem, now structural). And because multi-provider selection is punted to an unspecified operator layer, the clean "capability resolves to a provider" story has a hidden manual step that an ecosystem of overlapping third-party plugins will hit immediately. This is the difference between "negotiation is the moat" and "negotiation is a coarse hint plus operator glue."
- **Freeze-reopen?** No — adding an optional `attributes`/`selector` to `capabilityRef` is *additive* (existing manifests still validate). The accurate framing (agreed with architect): it's **not "reopen the freeze," it's "spend a pre-publication design slot now or pay a flag-day later"** — retrofitting attribute-matching across a *populated* ecosystem is precisely the orthogonality-assumption flag-day ADR-003 exists to prevent.
- **Cross-lens:** **architect RAISED this and cross-cites it from the wire side (their F6).** Joint framing we converged on: the negotiation contract resolves **eligibility, not selection** — `capabilityRef` has no discriminator and the plane-binding layer isn't a frozen artifact, so the wire tells you *which providers could serve* but not *which one will*. Same seam, two faces: architect flags the wire-correctness side, I flag the author-blame/adoption side (coarse-match over-promise → the consuming plugin's name eats the blame). My ecosystem read: **structural-leaning** — "it says compatible but it explodes" is a known trust-erosion onramp, and it partially undermines the headline differentiator.
- **Suggested direction:** Decide the granularity story now: either (a) a thin, optional `attributes` map on `capabilityRef` (additive) that the marketplace/reconciler match on, or (b) an explicit, documented "capabilities are coarse; operator plane-bindings are the selection contract" position — and if (b), freeze the plane-binding shape as a real artifact, because today it's the load-bearing selection mechanism with no contract.

### F4 — The conformance-trust chain is not closed end-to-end (issuance + marketplace oracle are paper)
- **Severity:** High
- **Area:** operability|ecosystem
- **Finding:** Precisely scoped with the maintainer to avoid double-flagging the parts that *are* built. **Built and real:** core-side, at plugin load, D4 is enforced — `core/registry/verified.go` `NewVerified` REFUSES any plugin that `provides` a capability unless it carries an attestation that verifies against the core's authority keyring and covers every provided capability (`core/conformance/attestation.go`, ed25519). So "the core trusts self-asserted `provides`" is **not** a valid finding — credit the team. **Still paper (the real gap):** (i) **nothing in production issues the attestation** the core consumes — `conformance.Sign` is a test/tooling helper; there is no production conformance-runner that runs an axis's golden vectors → signs the result → publishes it; (ii) the **marketplace** listing's `conformed_capabilities` is self-asserted free text, the community ref hardcodes `conformed = provided`, and nothing verifies a signature at discovery/install time (`marketplace/v1/CONTRACT.md:57-62`); (iii) the manifest `trust`/signing block is optional-at-solo / deferred (`plugin.v1.json:150-171`; ADR-011-supply-chain backlog).
- **The trust gate is also TIERED (architect-supplied nuance):** conformance is *not* a universal hard wall — the `trust` block is "OPTIONAL at the solo tier (unsigned local plugins allowed); REQUIRED by team+ deployments" (`plugin.v1.json:150-153`). So an author *can* `chmod +x` and run an unconformant plugin locally (low activation energy to start); the gate is the price of being trusted by *someone else's* team deployment. That's the right LSP-vs-CSI middle path. **But** the schema's own STATUS note still reads "manifest `provides`/`conformed_capabilities` are currently self-asserted (no enforcer exists yet)" (`plugin.v1.json:6`) — which sits in visible tension with the maintainer's built `core/registry/verified.go` enforcement. The reconciliation: the core-load *check* exists, but with no issuance pipeline and the schema doc still describing a self-asserted world, the "soft-now, hard-later" tiering is a **promise, not a shipped end-to-end gate** — and the schema-comment-vs-verified.go drift is a concrete fixable inconsistency.
- **Why it matters:** The author's reach is gated by who will *install*. The chain that would make a third-party plugin both safe and discoverable — author → conformance-runner signs → marketplace verifies at install → core verifies at load — is **broken in the middle**: the core demands an attestation that no production tool yet produces, and the marketplace presents a `conformed` field it never checks. A self-asserted `conformed` badge is *worse than none* if surfaced as trust: it teaches users to trust a number the author typed. Sharper still (architect): **author #1 pays the conformance cost for a trust signal that nothing currently checks end-to-end** — the worst of both worlds for activation energy. Teams/enterprises — the paying, plugin-hungry consumers — will not install on that basis, which collapses the author's incentive to publish.
- **Freeze-reopen?** no (the attestation message + issuance + marketplace check are additive enrichments; `roadmap/backlog.md:23` already lists the signed-conformance-attestation message as additive)
- **Cross-lens:** **maintainer self-corrected** — their orientation note said "declared==conformed is enforced via signed attestation," which holds only for the core-load half; they confirm issuance + marketplace oracle are unbuilt and endorsed this exact scoping. **architect added** the soft-solo/hard-team+ tiering + the `plugin.v1.json:6`-vs-`verified.go` drift, concluding net-HELP *if* the tiering ships but "a promise, not a shipped gate" today. Plus a governance seam (see F6): *who runs the single conformance authority?* is undesigned (backlog E8).
- **Suggested direction:** Build the conformance-runner→signer→publish pipeline as a P1 ecosystem deliverable (not Phase 4); until then, render marketplace `conformed_capabilities` as *unverified/self-declared*, and make signing default (not optional) for team+ before soliciting any third-party listing.

### F5 — Cold-start has no wedge: the celebrated properties are maintainer benefits, not acquisition levers
- **Severity:** High (largely acknowledged — Phase-4 GTM)
- **Area:** premise|ecosystem
- **Finding:** Zero third-party plugins exist; ~32 are first-party. The vision's own anti-goal concedes architecture-push fails ("No new plugin axis ships in year 1 until 100 real users run the core daily"; `docs/vision.md:96`). The properties the design is proudest of — any-language (ADR-006), capability negotiation, no blessed vendor (`docs/vision.md:46-54`) — are real and better than dbt/VSCode, but every one is felt *after* an author decides to build and *after* a deployment exists to plug into. None answers "why write a RAT `format` plugin this quarter instead of a dbt adapter or Airflow provider?" — for which the only durable answers are *reach* (installers) and *glue saved*. Reach is zero; glue-saved only materializes once a deployment base exists.
- **Why it matters:** "Users → demand → plugins" is the real causality; "extensibility → plugins → users" is the trap (reviews/05 Element 1, confirmed). With no wedge axis, author #1 faces the F1/F2 friction and F3/F4 uninstallable-or-untrusted output for an audience of zero — the precise shape of "another OSGi" the docs name (`docs/vision.md:81`).
- **Freeze-reopen?** no (GTM/sequencing)
- **Cross-lens:** **maintainer agrees it's an open door** (named modal failure, Phase-4, no owner) and asked for my angle. My angle: the maintainer-benefit-≠-acquisition-lever framing + naming a concrete wedge (below) rather than treating "16+ axes" as traction.
- **Suggested direction:** Name one wedge axis and seed it hand-to-hand. Strongest candidate from the artifacts: `format`/`catalog` on the Iceberg/Delta tailwind (`docs/vision.md:36-40`) — an author who already maintains a format library gets a new distribution channel at near-zero marginal integration *iff* F1–F4 are fixed. Write the first three third-party plugins *with* design partners; treat that as the product, not the 19th axis.

### F6 — Governance: one vendor controls the contract, the registry, the marketplace, AND the single conformance authority
- **Severity:** Medium
- **Area:** premise|ecosystem
- **Finding:** The axis set is open (`kind` pattern; `plugin.v1.json:16-46`) but unknown kinds "SHOULD be proposed via ADR," and the ADR process is wholly core-team-owned (`docs/architecture/adrs/CLAUDE.md`). The `rat://` namespace, the golden vectors that define "correct," the reference marketplace, the freeze authority — and now (per F4) the **conformance authority keyring** the core trusts — all sit with one party. The maintainer concedes a single conformance authority is "a centralization/bottleneck that sits awkwardly with the 'no blessed anything' ethos," and trust-root governance/key-rotation is undesigned (backlog E8). There is no neutral-governance signal (foundation, open RFC process, independent conformance authority) of the kind that let Terraform's registry, K8s API-review, or CNCF projects earn third-party distribution bets.
- **Why it matters:** A serious third party betting distribution is underwriting open-core rug-pull risk *and* a single-keyholder trust root: today's Apache-2.0 contract + community marketplace + lone conformance signer can be re-licensed, re-governed, or used to bless a first-party plugin over a community peer. The design *says* first-party and third-party are peers (a real strength) but nothing *structurally* guarantees it. Post-Terraform-BSL/Redis/Elastic, authors ask this before contract elegance.
- **Freeze-reopen?** no (governance, not wire)
- **Cross-lens:** **maintainer conceded** the single-authority centralization tension (F4 follow-on); architect's plane-binding observation (F3) compounds it — the unspecified selection layer is also vendor-controlled.
- **Suggested direction:** Publish a short governance commitment before recruiting external authors: who proposes a `rat://` axis/capability, how a community plugin contests a first-party one, the trust-root model for conformance authorities (plural, federated?), and a stability/relicense pledge for the contract + reference marketplace.

### F7 — Two version axes + manifest↔image coupling is author bookkeeping friction
- **Severity:** Low
- **Area:** contracts|ecosystem
- **Finding:** An author tracks two version concepts — the plugin's SemVer (`metadata.version`, `plugin.v1.json:59-63`) and the capability major in every `rat://…/v1/…` URI (ADR-002 D4) — plus `compatible_core` as a third compatibility dimension (`plugin.v1.json:88-97`). D6 couples manifest to image, so a manifest-only fix (a typo, a corrected `support_url`, an added `suggests`) cannot ship without re-releasing the image.
- **Why it matters:** Minor individually; the sum is real load on the maintenance treadmill, and manifest↔image coupling means trivial metadata corrections force a full re-release — the papercut that nudges long-tail plugins toward "last updated 2 years ago."
- **Freeze-reopen?** no
- **Cross-lens:** none.
- **Suggested direction:** A crisp "versioning for authors" CONTRACT.md section (the kubelet/apiserver-skew analog from the author's vantage); consider a manifest sidecar resolvable without re-imaging, traded against D6's integrity guarantee.

## What's strong (calibration — not findings)
1. **The contract triple is best-in-class author ergonomics** — 1:1 capability↔method, minimal-mandatory-core per-kind schemas, capability-not-implementation coupling. Cleaner than VSCode/dbt.
2. **The per-axis `CONTRACT.md` guides exist and are good** — author-facing prose alongside the wire (error model, idempotency/crash-safety obligations, reference tables). The thing reviews/02 most feared missing.
3. **Conformance is real and shared, and core-load enforcement is genuinely built** — `make conformance` auto-discovers references against one golden-vector file; ADR-003's two-divergent-references rule makes the data-plane contracts battle-tested; and `NewVerified` actually refuses unattested `provides` (the issuance pipeline is the gap, not the check). The honesty of the `$comment`/status banners about what's *not* yet enforced is itself a trust signal to an evaluating author.
